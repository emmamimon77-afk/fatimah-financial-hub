Riba Explained

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define riba and understand its different types
  • Explain why interest is prohibited in Islam
  • Identify riba in modern financial products
  • Recognize halal alternatives to interest-based transactions

📖 What is Riba?

Riba (ربا) literally means "increase" or "growth". In Islamic terminology, it refers to any predetermined excess or interest charged on a loan or exchange of goods.

📚 Two Main Types of Riba

  • Riba Al-Nasi'ah: Interest on loans - predetermined excess charged for deferring payment
  • Riba Al-Fadl: Excess in exchange - unequal exchange of the same commodity

⚖️ Why is Riba Prohibited?

1. Economic Justice

Riba creates wealth without effort or risk-bearing, leading to inequality. The lender is guaranteed returns regardless of the borrower's outcome.

2. Social Compassion

Islam encourages lending to those in need as an act of charity (qard hasan), not as a profit-making opportunity.

3. Risk Sharing

Islam promotes partnership where profits and losses are shared, creating a more balanced and fair economic system.

💳 Riba in Modern Finance

Product/Transaction Ruling Explanation
Conventional Mortgage 🔴 HARAM Interest charged on home loan
Credit Card Interest 🔴 HARAM Charging interest on unpaid balance
Savings Account Interest 🔴 HARAM Bank pays you interest on deposits
Margin Trading 🔴 HARAM Borrowing money with interest to trade
Islamic Sukuk 🟢 HALAL Asset-backed, profit-sharing structure
Islamic Mortgage 🟢 HALAL Murabaha or Ijara structure (sale/lease)

💰 How Riba Appears in Trading

⛔ To Avoid

  • Margin Accounts: Borrowing with interest
  • Short Selling: Interest-like fees
  • CFDs: Interest on overnight positions
  • Forex Swaps: Interest charges
  • Options/Futures: Deferred payments

✅ Permitted

  • Cash accounts only
  • Spot transactions
  • Profit-sharing accounts
  • Islamic ETFs
  • Sukuk
