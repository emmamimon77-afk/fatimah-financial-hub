Understanding Economic Indicators: Step-by-Step

📊 Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

Step 1: Calculate GDP = Consumption + Investment + Government Spending + (Exports - Imports)

Step 2: Compare current vs previous quarter: (Q2 - Q1) / Q1 × 100

Step 3: Annualize: Multiply quarterly rate by 4

Real-world: 2-3% growth = healthy; below 0% = recession

📈 Inflation (CPI)

Step 1: Bureau of Labor Statistics tracks 94,000 prices monthly

Step 2: CPI = (Current Basket Cost / Base Year Basket Cost) × 100

Step 3: Inflation Rate = (CPI Year2 - CPI Year1) / CPI Year1 × 100

Real-world: Fed targets 2% average inflation

👥 Unemployment Rate

Step 1: Labor Force = Employed + Unemployed (actively seeking)

Step 2: Unemployment Rate = Unemployed / Labor Force × 100

Step 3: Calculate U-6 (includes discouraged workers) for broader measure

Real-world: U-3 reported rate; U-6 typically 3-4% higher

Okun's Law (GDP-unemployment)

Philips Curve: π = πᵉ - β(u - uⁿ) + ε
π = inflation, πᵉ = expected inflation, u = unemployment, uⁿ = natural rate
Okun's Law: (Y - Y*) / Y* = -c (u - u*)
Y = actual GDP, Y* = potential GDP, u = actual unemployment, u* = natural rate, c ≈ 2
Leading Indicators Predict future economic activity: stock market, building permits, consumer confidence
Lagging Indicators Confirm trends after they occur: unemployment rate, corporate profits, labor costs
Coincident Indicators Move with the economy: GDP, industrial production, retail sales
Core CPI CPI excluding food and energy - less volatile, better for trend analysis

Economic Insights & Applications

  • GDP & Markets: 2 consecutive quarters negative GDP = recession (conventional definition)
  • Inflation & Bonds: Rising inflation causes bond prices to fall, yields to rise
  • Unemployment & Fed: Fed raises rates when unemployment falls below natural rate to prevent overheating
  • Philips Curve Today: Relationship has weakened; "flattened" curve means inflation less responsive to unemployment

Key Formula: Economic Growth = f(Capital, Labor, Technology) - Solow Growth Model (Nobel Prize 1987)
