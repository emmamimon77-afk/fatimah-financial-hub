Supply and Demand

Beginner • 35 min read
12 Quiz Questions Lesson 2 of 20 Includes Islamic Perspective
وَأَحَلَّ اللَّهُ الْبَيْعَ وَحَرَّمَ الرِّبَا
"And Allah has permitted trade and forbidden interest."
— Quran 2:275

Trade (al-bay') is the exchange of goods based on mutual consent. Supply and demand is simply the study of how this exchange happens in markets.

The Heart of Market Economics

Supply and demand is the most fundamental concept in economics. It explains how prices are determined, how resources are allocated, and why some goods are expensive while others are cheap.

📚 The Law of Supply and Demand: Prices are determined by the interaction between how much of a good is available (supply) and how much people want it (demand). When demand exceeds supply, prices rise. When supply exceeds demand, prices fall.

Demand: The Consumer's Side

Demand refers to how much of a good or service consumers are willing and able to buy at different prices.

High Price → Low Demand Low Price → High Demand

The Law of Demand: When prices go up, quantity demanded goes down. When prices go down, quantity demanded goes up.

Example: Coffee Shop

If a cup of coffee costs $5, you might buy one cup per day. If it costs $10, you might buy only on special occasions. If it costs $1, you might buy two cups. This is the law of demand in action.

Psychology of Demand

Demand reflects human desire and utility. We want things that give us satisfaction (utility). But we're limited by our budget. The downward slope of demand represents our diminishing enthusiasm—the first cup of coffee is wonderful, the fourth cup much less so.

Islamic Insight: Islam encourages moderation in consumption. "Eat and drink, but be not excessive." (Quran 7:31). Demand should reflect genuine need, not wasteful extravagance.

Supply: The Producer's Side

Supply refers to how much of a good or service producers are willing and able to sell at different prices.

Low Price → Low Supply High Price → High Supply

The Law of Supply: When prices go up, quantity supplied goes up. When prices go down, quantity supplied goes down.

Example: Baker

A baker can sell bread for $2 per loaf and makes 100 loaves. If the price rises to $4 per loaf, it's worth hiring extra help and working longer hours to make 200 loaves. Higher prices incentivize more production.

Psychology of Supply

Supply reflects producer motivation. Higher prices mean higher potential profits, which motivates producers to work harder, invest more, and take more risks. The upward slope represents increasing costs—producing the 100th loaf costs more than the first due to overtime, machine wear, etc.

Islamic Insight: The Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) said: "The truthful and honest merchant will be with the prophets, the truthful, and the martyrs." (Tirmidhi). Supply should be honest—accurate weights, fair prices, transparent quality.

Market Equilibrium: When Supply Meets Demand
Demand Supply Equilibrium

Equilibrium is the price where the quantity consumers want to buy exactly equals the quantity producers want to sell. At this price, there is no shortage and no surplus.

Price ($) Quantity Demanded Quantity Supplied Market Condition
$10 50 150 Surplus (excess supply)
$7 100 100 ⚖️ EQUILIBRIUM
$5 150 60 Shortage (excess demand)

How Markets Reach Equilibrium

If price is too high ($10): Sellers have 150 items but buyers only want 50. Surplus! Sellers lower prices to attract buyers. Price falls toward $7.

If price is too low ($5): Buyers want 150 items but sellers only have 60. Shortage! Buyers bid prices up. Price rises toward $7.

At equilibrium ($7): Buyers get what they want, sellers sell what they make. No pressure to change price.

Islamic Perspective on Pricing

The Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) was asked to fix prices when they rose in Medina. He refused, saying: "Allah is the One Who fixes prices, Who withholds, Who gives lavishly, and Who provides. I hope to meet my Lord with none of you having a claim against me for an injustice concerning blood or property." (Abu Dawud)

This teaches us that market prices should be determined by natural supply and demand, not artificial controls—as long as there's no manipulation, hoarding, or fraud. Prices are a trust from Allah.

Movements vs Shifts

📉 Movement Along the Curve

Caused by: Change in price

  • Price goes up → move up the demand curve (buy less)
  • Price goes down → move down the demand curve (buy more)

📈 Shift of the Curve

Caused by: Change in other factors

  • Income changes
  • Preferences change
  • Population changes
  • Price of related goods

Understanding the Difference

Movement: Coffee price rises from $3 to $4 → you buy less coffee. You're moving along the same demand curve.

Shift: You get a promotion and higher salary → you buy more coffee at every price. The entire demand curve shifts right.

What Shifts Demand?
💰 Income: Higher income → more demand for normal goods (restaurants, travel). Lower income → more demand for inferior goods (instant noodles, public transport).
❤️ Preferences/Tastes: Health trends increase demand for organic food, decrease demand for sugary drinks.
👥 Population: More people → more demand for everything.
🔄 Price of Related Goods:
  • Substitutes: Tea price rises → demand for coffee rises (people switch)
  • Complements: Phone price rises → demand for phone cases falls (used together)
What Shifts Supply?
🏭 Technology: Better machines → can produce more at same cost → supply shifts right.
💰 Input Prices: Cheaper materials → more profitable → supply shifts right. Higher wages → less profitable → supply shifts left.
👥 Number of Sellers: More firms enter market → supply shifts right.
🌧️ Natural Conditions: Good weather → crop supply increases. Bad weather → supply decreases.
⚖️ Government Policy: Taxes decrease supply, subsidies increase supply.
Real-World Examples

📱 iPhone Launch

Demand: High (fans want new phone). Supply: Limited initially. Result: High prices, long lines, scalpers.

🌾 Crop Harvest

Good harvest: Supply increases → prices fall. Bad harvest: Supply decreases → prices rise.

⛽ Oil Prices

Political instability in oil-producing countries → supply decreases → prices rise worldwide.

🏠 Housing Market

Population grows in a city → demand increases → prices rise. Builders see opportunity → supply increases over time.

Test Your Understanding

Answer these 12 questions to check your understanding. Click on the correct answer to see feedback.

1. According to the law of demand, when price increases, quantity demanded:

A. Increases
B. Decreases
C. Stays the same
D. Doubles
✅ Correct! The law of demand states an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded. Higher price → lower demand.

2. What happens at the equilibrium price?

A. There is a shortage
B. There is a surplus
C. Quantity demanded equals quantity supplied
D. Price is at its maximum
✅ Correct! At equilibrium, the market clears—buyers want exactly what sellers are offering. No shortage, no surplus.

3. If the price is set below equilibrium, what results?

A. Surplus
B. Shortage
C. Perfect balance
D. Increased supply
✅ Correct! Below equilibrium, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied, creating a shortage. Think of concert tickets sold too cheap—long lines and scalpers.

4. Which factor would cause a shift in the demand curve (not just a movement along it)?

A. The price of the good changes
B. Consumer income increases
C. The price goes up
D. The price goes down
✅ Correct! Income changes shift the entire demand curve. Price changes only cause movement along the curve.

5. If coffee and tea are substitutes, what happens when coffee prices rise?

A. Demand for tea increases
B. Demand for tea decreases
C. Demand for coffee increases
D. No change
✅ Correct! Substitutes are alternatives. When coffee gets expensive, people switch to tea, increasing tea demand.

6. What does the law of supply state?

A. Price and quantity supplied are inversely related
B. Price and quantity supplied are directly related
C. Supply is fixed regardless of price
D. Higher prices reduce supply
✅ Correct! Higher prices incentivize producers to supply more. Supply curve slopes upward.

7. A new technology makes smartphones cheaper to produce. What happens to supply?

A. Supply decreases (shifts left)
B. Supply increases (shifts right)
C. Supply stays the same
D. Price increases
✅ Correct! Better technology reduces costs, making production more profitable at every price. Supply curve shifts right.

8. What did the Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) say when people asked him to fix rising prices?

A. He immediately set price controls
B. He blamed the merchants
C. He refused, saying Allah alone fixes prices
D. He lowered taxes
✅ Correct! The Prophet refused to fix prices, teaching that market prices should reflect natural supply and demand without artificial interference.

9. If the population of a city grows rapidly, what happens to the demand for housing?

A. Demand increases (shifts right)
B. Demand decreases (shifts left)
C. Demand stays the same
D. Supply increases
✅ Correct! More people means more housing needed at every price point. The entire demand curve shifts right.

10. Which is an example of complementary goods?

A. Coffee and tea
B. Pepsi and Coke
C. Printers and ink cartridges
D. Beef and chicken
✅ Correct! Complements are goods used together. If printer prices rise, demand for ink falls because fewer people buy printers.

11. If a drought destroys wheat crops, what happens to the price of bread?

A. Price increases
B. Price decreases
C. Price stays the same
D. Price becomes zero
✅ Correct! Drought reduces supply of wheat (shift left), which increases the price of bread made from wheat.

12. In Islamic economics, what is the ruling on hoarding goods to drive up prices?

A. Permissible - it's good business
B. Encouraged - maximizes profit
C. Prohibited (haram) - it manipulates supply
D. Neutral - no ruling
✅ Correct! Hoarding (ihtikar) to manipulate prices is prohibited in Islam. The Prophet (ﷺ) said: "Whoever hoards food for forty days, he has disassociated himself from Allah and Allah has disassociated from him." (Ahmad)
