What is Economics?

Beginner • 30 min read

"And do not consume one another's wealth unjustly."

Economics at its core is about how we manage resources justly. Allah reminds us that wealth must be earned and exchanged fairly.

What is Economics?

Economics is the study of how people make choices when faced with scarcity. It's the science of decision-making—how individuals, businesses, and governments allocate limited resources to satisfy unlimited wants.

📚 Formal Definition: "Economics is the social science that studies the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services."

The word "economics" comes from the Greek oikonomia meaning "household management." In Arabic, it's related to iqtisad (اقتصاد) meaning moderation and balance—not too much, not too little.

The Fundamental Problem: Scarcity

We live in a world of limited resources but unlimited wants. This gap between what we have and what we want is the fundamental economic problem.

📦 Limited Resources

  • Time (24 hours a day)
  • Money (budget constraints)
  • Natural resources (oil, water, land)
  • Labor (skilled workers)
  • Capital (machinery, factories)

✨ Unlimited Wants

  • Better food and clothing
  • Bigger homes and cars
  • Education and healthcare
  • Travel and entertainment
  • Security and comfort

Real-Life Example

You have $100 and 2 hours free time. You want to buy groceries, see a movie, and have dinner with friends. You cannot do everything. Economics helps you decide how to allocate your limited money and time to maximize your satisfaction.

Human Psychology

Scarcity creates trade-offs. Every choice means giving up something else. This is called opportunity cost—the value of the next best alternative you forgo. When you choose one thing, you're saying "no" to everything else.

Quranic Insight: "And do not extend your eyes toward that by which We have given enjoyment to various categories of them [the disbelievers]—the splendor of worldly life by which We test them." (Quran 20:131). Allah reminds us that we cannot have everything; we are tested by what we desire but cannot have.

Every Economy Must Answer Three Questions
❓

What to produce?

Given limited resources, which goods and services should we create?

⚙️

How to produce?

What methods, technology, and labor should we use?

👥

For whom to produce?

Who gets to consume the goods and services?

Example: A Farmer's Decisions

What? Should I grow wheat or raise cattle?
How? Should I use traditional methods or modern machinery?
For whom? Should I sell at local market or export internationally?

Two Branches of Economics

🔍 Microeconomics

Studies individual units:

  • Households and consumers
  • Firms and businesses
  • Individual markets (e.g., housing market)
  • Supply and demand for specific goods
  • Price determination

Example: How does a price increase affect your purchase of coffee?

🌍 Macroeconomics

Studies the economy as a whole:

  • Gross Domestic Product (GDP)
  • Inflation and unemployment
  • Economic growth
  • Monetary and fiscal policy
  • International trade

Example: How does inflation affect the entire country's purchasing power?

Islamic Perspective

Islamic economics combines both micro and macro concerns with a moral framework. At the micro level, individuals must avoid riba (interest) and gharar (uncertainty). At the macro level, the state must ensure zakat is collected and distributed, and that wealth doesn't concentrate among the rich.

Quranic Foundation: "So that it will not be a perpetual distribution among the rich from among you." (Quran 59:7)

Positive vs Normative Economics

📊 Positive Economics

Deals with facts and "what is"

  • Objective and testable
  • Can be proven true or false
  • Example: "Unemployment is 5%"
  • Example: "Increasing minimum wage reduces employment"

⚖️ Normative Economics

Deals with values and "what ought to be"

  • Subjective and opinion-based
  • Cannot be proven true or false
  • Example: "Unemployment should be lower"
  • Example: "The government should raise minimum wage"

Understanding the Difference

Positive: "A 10% minimum wage increase leads to 2% job loss among teenagers." (This can be tested with data)

Normative: "The government should increase minimum wage to help poor families." (This is an opinion based on values)

Types of Economic Systems

🏛️ Traditional Economy

Custom and tradition determine what, how, and for whom to produce. Found in rural, agricultural societies. Example: Indigenous communities, remote villages.

📈 Market Economy (Capitalism)

Decisions made by individuals and firms through markets. Supply and demand determine prices. Example: United States, UK.

🏭 Command Economy (Communism)

Government makes all economic decisions. Central planning determines production and distribution. Example: North Korea, former Soviet Union.

🕌 Islamic Economic System

A middle path combining market mechanisms with moral filters. Permits private ownership and profit but prohibits interest (riba), excessive uncertainty (gharar), and harmful industries. Mandates zakat (2.5% wealth tax) for social welfare.

Why Study Economics?

💼 Career Benefits

  • Banking and finance
  • Government and policy
  • Business management
  • Consulting and analytics
  • International development

🧠 Personal Benefits

  • Make better financial decisions
  • Understand news and current events
  • Evaluate government policies
  • Think critically about trade-offs
  • Understand global markets

Islamic Perspective

Studying economics helps Muslims fulfill their religious obligations. You cannot avoid riba if you don't understand how it works. You cannot give proper zakat without understanding wealth calculation. You cannot judge whether a company is halal without understanding its business model.

Prophetic Wisdom: The Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) said: "The trustworthy, honest Muslim merchant will be with the martyrs on the Day of Resurrection." (Ibn Majah). Economics helps you become that honest merchant.

Test Your Understanding

Answer these 10 questions to check your understanding. Click on the correct answer to see feedback.

1. What is the fundamental economic problem?

A. Scarcity - limited resources vs unlimited wants
B. Inflation - rising prices over time
C. Unemployment - people without jobs
D. Inequality - rich vs poor gap
✅ Correct! Scarcity is the fundamental problem. All other economic issues (inflation, unemployment, inequality) arise from how we deal with scarcity.

2. What is opportunity cost?

A. The monetary cost of a purchase
B. The value of the next best alternative you give up
C. The total cost of production
D. The price you pay at the store
✅ Correct! Opportunity cost is what you sacrifice when you make a choice. If you spend $20 on a movie, your opportunity cost is the dinner you could have bought instead.

3. Which branch of economics studies individual markets and consumer behavior?

A. Microeconomics
B. Macroeconomics
C. Normative economics
D. Positive economics
✅ Correct! Microeconomics focuses on individual units - households, firms, and specific markets. Macroeconomics looks at the entire economy.

4. "The unemployment rate is 5%" is an example of:

A. Positive economics
B. Normative economics
C. Microeconomics
D. Islamic economics
✅ Correct! This is a positive statement because it's a fact that can be verified with data. Normative statements involve opinions like "unemployment should be lower."

5. Which economic system prohibits interest (riba) and mandates zakat?

A. Market economy
B. Command economy
C. Traditional economy
D. Islamic economic system
✅ Correct! Islamic economics combines market mechanisms with moral filters - prohibiting riba, gharar, and haram industries while mandating zakat for social welfare.

6. The three basic economic questions are: What to produce? How to produce? and:

A. Why to produce?
B. When to produce?
C. For whom to produce?
D. Where to produce?
✅ Correct! Every economy must decide WHAT goods to make, HOW to make them, and WHO gets to consume them.

7. Which Quranic verse relates to fair economic transactions?

A. "Do not consume one another's wealth unjustly" (2:188)
B. "And do not kill yourselves" (4:29)
C. "And speak to people kindly" (2:83)
D. "And establish prayer" (2:43)
✅ Correct! Quran 2:188 specifically prohibits consuming wealth unjustly, which is the foundation of Islamic economic ethics.

8. If you have 2 hours and $50, and you choose to watch a movie instead of having dinner with friends, what is your opportunity cost?

A. The $50 you spent on the movie
B. The 2 hours of your time
C. The enjoyment of dinner with friends
D. Both A and B
✅ Correct! Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative - in this case, the enjoyment of dinner with friends. The money and time are just resources; the real cost is what you give up experiencing.

9. What does the Arabic word "iqtisad" (اقتصاد) imply about economics in Islamic thought?

A. Maximizing profits
B. Moderation and balance - the middle path
C. Unlimited growth
D. Government control
✅ Correct! Iqtisad means moderation, balance, and the middle path. Islamic economics is not about extreme wealth or poverty, but about balanced, ethical management of resources.

10. Why should Muslims study economics according to the lesson?

A. Only to get rich
B. To pass exams
C. To fulfill religious obligations (avoid riba, calculate zakat, identify halal investments)
D. To impress friends
✅ Correct! Muslims study economics to understand how to earn and spend in halal ways, avoid riba, calculate zakat correctly, and make ethical investment decisions.
