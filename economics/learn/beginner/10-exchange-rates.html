Exchange rates remind us that the value of things changes. In all transactions, justice and fairness must prevail.

What is an Exchange Rate?

An exchange rate is simply the price of one currency in terms of another. Just as apples have a price in dollars, currencies have prices in other currencies.

📚 Simple Definition: If 1 US Dollar buys 3.75 Saudi Riyals, the exchange rate is 3.75 SAR/USD. If you travel from USA to Saudi Arabia, each of your dollars becomes 3.75 riyals to spend.

$1 USD = 3.75 SAR 🇸🇦
$1 USD = 0.92 EUR 🇪🇺
$1 USD = 150 JPY 🇯🇵
$1 USD = 1.35 CAD 🇨🇦

These rates change constantly—sometimes by the second! In 2026, approximately $6.6 trillion worth of currencies are traded every single day.

How Exchange Rates Are Quoted

🇺🇸 Direct Quote

USD/currency - How much foreign currency per dollar

Example: USD/EUR = 0.92 means $1 = €0.92

🇪🇺 Indirect Quote

currency/USD - How many dollars per foreign currency

Example: EUR/USD = 1.09 means €1 = $1.09

Cross Rates

You can also calculate rates between two non-dollar currencies:

If $1 = €0.92 and $1 = ¥150, then €1 = ¥150 ÷ 0.92 = ¥163.04

Types of Exchange Rate Systems

📈 Floating (Market-Based)

Value determined by supply and demand

  • USA, Eurozone, Japan, UK
  • Changes constantly
  • Central banks may occasionally intervene

⚓ Fixed (Pegged)

Value tied to another currency or gold

  • Saudi Arabia (3.75 to USD)
  • Hong Kong (7.8 to USD)
  • Denmark (to Euro)
  • Provides stability for trade

🔄 Crawling Peg

Regularly adjusted fixed rate

China used this for years—gradually letting currency appreciate

💱 Managed Float

Mostly market-driven but with central bank intervention

Many emerging markets use this

Gulf Currencies

Saudi Riyal: Pegged at 3.75 to USD since 1986
UAE Dirham: Pegged at 3.67 to USD
Qatari Riyal: Pegged at 3.64 to USD
Kuwaiti Dinar: Pegged to a basket of currencies (strongest currency in the world!)

What Determines Exchange Rates?

📊 Economic Factors

  • Interest rates: Higher rates attract foreign investment → currency strengthens
  • Inflation: Higher inflation weakens currency (goods more expensive)
  • Trade balance: Surplus strengthens currency (foreigners need your currency to buy your goods)
  • Economic growth: Strong growth attracts investment → currency strengthens

🧠 Psychological Factors

  • Political stability: Safe havens (USD, CHF, gold) strengthen during uncertainty
  • Market sentiment: "Risk-on" vs "risk-off"
  • Speculation: Traders betting on future movements
  • News and events: Elections, wars, natural disasters

🏆 Robert Mundell • Nobel Prize 1999

Father of the theory of optimum currency areas. His work on exchange rates and monetary policy laid the foundation for the euro. The "Mundell-Fleming model" explains how monetary and fiscal policy work under different exchange rate regimes.

The Carry Trade - A Famous Strategy

Borrow in a currency with low interest rates (like Japanese Yen, near 0%), invest in a currency with high rates (like Australian Dollar, 4-5%). Profit from the difference. But if exchange rates move against you, losses can be massive—as many learned in 2008.

Effects of Exchange Rate Changes

📈 Strong Currency (Appreciation)

$1 buys more foreign currency

Good for: Importers, travelers, foreign debt payers

Bad for: Exporters (goods expensive abroad), tourism industry

📉 Weak Currency (Depreciation)

$1 buys less foreign currency

Good for: Exporters, tourism, domestic industries competing with imports

Bad for: Importers, travelers, those with foreign debt

Travel Example

2024: $1 = €1.10 → Your $5000 trip costs €5500 in Europe

2026: $1 = €0.92 → Your $5000 trip costs only €4600

You saved €900! A weaker dollar makes European travel more expensive for Americans; a stronger dollar makes it cheaper.

Business Example

BMW (German carmaker): When euro strengthens against dollar, BMW's cars become more expensive in America. Sales may fall. When euro weakens, BMW becomes more competitive.

Famous Exchange Rate Events

🇬🇧 1992: Black Wednesday

George Soros "broke the Bank of England." He bet £10 billion that the pound would fall. The UK was forced to withdraw from the European Exchange Rate Mechanism. Soros made $1 billion in one day.

🇦🇷 2001: Argentine Peso Collapse

After years of pegging 1 peso = 1 dollar, Argentina's economy collapsed. The peg broke, peso lost 70% of its value, causing widespread poverty and riots.

🇨🇭 2015: Swiss Franc Shock

Swiss National Bank suddenly removed its cap on franc versus euro. Franc surged 30% in minutes. Many brokers and traders bankrupted.

🇹🇷 2020-2025: Turkish Lira Crisis

Unconventional monetary policy (cutting rates despite high inflation) caused lira to lose 80% of its value against dollar. Turks' savings devastated.

The US Dollar: Global Reserve Currency

Since 1944 (Bretton Woods), the US dollar has been the world's primary reserve currency. About 60% of global central bank reserves are in dollars. Most international trade (especially oil) is priced in dollars.

✅ Benefits to USA

  • Can borrow cheaply (everyone wants dollars)
  • No exchange rate risk for US companies
  • Political power (sanctions possible)

⚠️ Costs to USA

  • Overvalued dollar hurts manufacturing
  • Must run trade deficits to supply dollars
  • Responsibility to maintain stability

🌍 De-dollarization?

Countries like China, Russia, and some Muslim nations are exploring alternatives—trading in yuan, rubles, or gold. The rise of digital currencies and geopolitical shifts may slowly reduce dollar dominance over coming decades.

Islamic Perspective on Currency Exchange

📜 Fiqh of Currency Exchange (Al-Sarf)

Islamic law has detailed rules for currency exchange to prevent riba and gharar:

  • Hand-to-hand (spot): Exchange must be completed immediately (no delayed settlement)
  • Equal if same currency: Exchanging dollars for dollars must be at par (no profit on same currency)
  • Different currencies: Can be at different rates, but must be spot transaction

⚠️ What's Prohibited

  • Forex speculation: Trading currencies with leverage, interest, or delayed settlement (common in conventional forex trading)
  • Currency futures/options: Most scholars consider them haram due to gharar and lack of asset backing
  • Interest-bearing accounts: Holding foreign currency in interest-bearing accounts

Halal Currency Exchange

✅ Travel exchange: You go to an exchange bureau, pay dollars, receive euros immediately. Halal.

✅ Islamic forex accounts: Some brokers offer "Islamic accounts" with no interest (swap-free) and spot-only trading. Permissible if no other violations.

✅ Currency as payment: Accepting different currencies for goods/services is fine.

Haram Currency Trading

❌ Speculative forex: Borrowing 100x leverage, paying interest on overnight positions, betting on price movements without any intention of actual currency use—this resembles gambling and involves riba.

Test Your Understanding

1. What is an exchange rate?

A. The interest rate set by central banks
B. The price of one currency in terms of another
C. The inflation rate
D. The stock market index
✅ Correct! Exchange rate is simply the price of one currency measured in another currency.

2. If 1 USD = 0.92 EUR, what is the EUR/USD rate?

A. 0.92
B. 1.09 (1 ÷ 0.92)
C. 1.00
D. 0.85
✅ Correct! If $1 = €0.92, then €1 = $1 ÷ 0.92 = $1.09. The reciprocal gives the other quote.

3. Which Gulf currency is pegged to the US dollar at approximately 3.75?

A. UAE Dirham
B. Qatari Riyal
C. Saudi Riyal
D. Kuwaiti Dinar
✅ Correct! Saudi Riyal has been pegged at 3.75 to USD since 1986, providing stability for oil trade.

4. What happens to a country's exports when its currency weakens?

A. Exports become cheaper for foreigners, so they increase
B. Exports become more expensive, so they decrease
C. Exports are unchanged
D. Exports stop completely
✅ Correct! A weaker currency makes your goods cheaper for foreigners to buy, boosting exports.

5. What is a "fixed" or "pegged" exchange rate?

A. Rate set by market supply and demand
B. Rate officially tied to another currency or gold
C. Rate that changes every minute
D. Rate set by the IMF
✅ Correct! Fixed rates are officially set and maintained by central banks, often tied to a major currency like USD.

6. Who won the Nobel Prize for work on optimum currency areas?

A. Milton Friedman
B. Paul Krugman
C. Robert Mundell
D. Joseph Stiglitz
✅ Correct! Robert Mundell won the Nobel Prize in 1999 for his work on exchange rates and optimum currency areas, laying the foundation for the euro.

7. What happened on "Black Wednesday" (1992)?

A. US stock market crashed
B. George Soros forced UK out of ERM, pound collapsed
C. Japanese yen hit record high
D. Euro was introduced
✅ Correct! George Soros famously "broke the Bank of England," forcing the UK out of the European Exchange Rate Mechanism and making $1 billion profit.

8. What is the Islamic ruling on currency exchange (Al-Sarf)?

A. Must be spot (hand-to-hand), no interest, equal if same currency
B. Any type of forex trading is allowed
C. Only gold can be exchanged
D. Delayed settlement is fine
✅ Correct! Islamic law requires spot (immediate) exchange, prohibits interest, and requires equal value when exchanging same currency.

9. Which currency is the world's primary reserve currency?

A. US Dollar
B. Euro
C. Japanese Yen
D. British Pound
✅ Correct! The US dollar is the world's primary reserve currency, with about 60% of central bank reserves held in dollars.

10. What does a "strong" currency mean for travelers?

A. Their money buys more abroad (cheaper travel)
B. Their money buys less abroad (expensive travel)
C. No change
D. They cannot travel
✅ Correct! A strong currency means each unit buys more foreign currency, making travel and imports cheaper.

11. What happened to Turkish Lira between 2020-2025?

A. Lost about 80% of its value due to unconventional policy
B. Became the world's strongest currency
C. Was pegged to euro
D. No change
✅ Correct! Turkey's experiment with low interest rates despite high inflation caused the lira to crash, devastating savings.

12. What is "de-dollarization"?

A. Countries reducing reliance on US dollar in trade and reserves
B. Abolishing the dollar completely
C. Dollar becoming stronger
D. Printing more dollars
✅ Correct! De-dollarization refers to countries (like China, Russia, some Muslim nations) trying to trade in other currencies to reduce dollar dependence.
