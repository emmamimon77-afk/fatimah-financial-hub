The Human Side of Economics

Traditional economics assumes "Homo economicus"—rational, self-interested, with perfect information. Behavioral economics studies how humans ACTUALLY behave: emotional, irrational, and wonderfully complicated.

📚 Behavioral Economics: The combination of psychology and economics that studies how cognitive biases, emotions, and social factors influence economic decisions.

🏆 Daniel Kahneman • Nobel Prize 2002

Psychologist who won Nobel in Economics. Pioneered study of cognitive biases with Amos Tversky. Showed how humans systematically make "irrational" decisions.

Thinking, Fast and Slow

⚡ System 1: Fast

  • Automatic, intuitive
  • No effort
  • Emotional
  • Hard to control
  • Examples: Recognize a face, drive on empty road

🧠 System 2: Slow

  • Deliberate, analytical
  • Requires effort
  • Logical
  • Lazy (tires easily)
  • Examples: Solve math problem, choose retirement plan

The Bat and Ball Problem

Question: A bat and ball cost $1.10. The bat costs $1 more than the ball. How much is the ball?

System 1 says: 10 cents! (Wrong!)

System 2 calculates: Ball = x, Bat = x+1, Total = 2x+1 = 1.10 → x = 5 cents

Most people answer 10 cents because System 1 is fast but wrong.

Common Cognitive Biases

1. Loss Aversion

Losing $100 hurts more than gaining $100 pleases. About 2-2.5x more painful. This explains why investors hold losing stocks too long (hope) and sell winners too early (fear).

2. Anchoring

We rely too heavily on first information. A $1000 suit seems reasonable after seeing a $2000 suit. Used in negotiations, sales, pricing.

3. Confirmation Bias

We seek information that confirms our beliefs, ignore contradicting evidence. In trading, you remember winning trades, forget losers.

4. Overconfidence

90% of drivers think they're above average. Traders think they can beat market (most can't). CEOs overestimate merger success.

5. Present Bias

We value today much more than tomorrow. Why we eat cake now, regret later. Why we don't save enough for retirement.

6. Herd Mentality

We follow others. Stock market bubbles, bank runs, fashion trends. "If everyone's buying, it must be good."

7. Framing Effect

"90% survival rate" sounds better than "10% mortality rate"—same information, different reaction. How choices are presented matters.

8. Endowment Effect

We value things more once we own them. Your coffee mug is worth more to you than the same mug in a store.

Prospect Theory: Kahneman & Tversky's Revolution

📊 The Experiment

Choice 1: A) Guaranteed $900 B) 90% chance of $1000, 10% chance of $0

Most choose A (risk-averse for gains).

Choice 2: A) Guaranteed loss $900 B) 90% chance of losing $1000, 10% chance of losing $0

Most choose B (risk-seeking for losses).

This contradicts traditional economics (which says both should be same).

📈 Value Function

  • Steeper for losses than gains
  • Diminishing sensitivity (difference between $0 and $100 feels bigger than $900 and $1000)
  • Explains why we're risk-averse with gains, risk-seeking with losses

⚖️ Weighting Function

  • We overweigh small probabilities (lottery tickets, insurance)
  • We underweigh medium/large probabilities
  • Why we fear plane crashes (rare) more than car crashes (common)
Mental Accounting (Richard Thaler)

🏆 Richard Thaler • Nobel Prize 2017

Father of behavioral economics. Showed how people mentally categorize money differently, leading to irrational decisions.

The $100 Suit

You have $100. You plan to buy a suit for $95 and a book for $5. At the store, you discover the suit is on sale for $90. Do you buy it? Most say yes—you save $5!

But what if you're buying the book first, and it's on sale from $5 to $2? Most say yes again. But mathematically it's the same $5 saving!

Mental accounting: we treat money in different "accounts" (suit account, book account) instead of integrating.

💳 Credit Card Premium

People will pay more with credit card than cash for same item—because mental accounting separates payment from consumption. Credit "feels" less painful.

Nudge: Libertarian Paternalism

📚 Richard Thaler & Cass Sunstein • "Nudge" (2008)

Small changes in "choice architecture" can dramatically improve decisions—without restricting freedom.

🍏 Cafeteria Layout

Place healthy food at eye level, junk food harder to reach. Sales of healthy items increase 25%—without banning anything.

💰 Save More Tomorrow

Employees commit now to save more from FUTURE pay raises (not current income). Loss aversion avoided. Savings rates tripled.

⚙️ Defaults Matter

Opt-out organ donation (default = donor) vs opt-in (default = non-donor). Countries with opt-out have 90%+ donation rates; opt-in have 15%.

📊 Pension Enrollment

Automatic enrollment (opt-out) boosts participation from 40% to 90%+ compared to opt-in.

Behavioral Finance

📈 Disposition Effect

Investors sell winners too early (realize gains) and hold losers too long (avoid realizing losses). Loss aversion at work.

📊 Herding in Bubbles

Dot-com bubble (1999), Housing bubble (2006), Crypto frenzy (2021). Everyone buys because everyone's buying. Then panic.

📉 Overtrading

Men trade 45% more than women, earn 1% less. Overconfidence leads to excessive trading. "Buy and hold" beats active trading for most.

🧠 Home Bias

Investors overweight domestic stocks. Saudis invest in Saudi, Americans in US—even when diversification would reduce risk.

Islamic Perspective: Knowing the Self

📜 The Three Selves in Islam

  • Nafs al-Ammarah (the commanding self): Prone to evil, follows desires, impulsive. Behavioral biases come from here.
  • Nafs al-Lawwama (the blaming self): Conscience that regrets, feels guilt after wrong decisions.
  • Nafs al-Mutma'innah (the peaceful self): Attained through discipline, prayer, self-control—the ideal.

⚠️ Biases as Nafs al-Ammarah

  • Greed: Hoarding, excessive risk
  • Fear: Panic selling, avoiding charity
  • Herd instinct: Following without thought
  • Overconfidence: Forgetting Allah's role

✅ Solutions in Islam

  • Muhasaba: Self-accounting, reflection
  • Tawakkul: Trust in Allah reduces anxiety
  • Sabr: Patience counters loss aversion
  • Shura: Consultation counters overconfidence
  • Dhikr: Remembrance brings peace

Islamic Nudges

🕌 Zakat as Nudge

Automatic 2.5% wealth redistribution. Set by Allah—no choice. But also nudges: pay in Ramadan for more reward, pay secretly to avoid showing off.

🕋 Hajj Savings

Many Muslims save specifically for Hajj (mental accounting). Islamic banks offer dedicated Hajj accounts—positive nudge.

🍖 Qurbani (Sacrifice)

Annual reminder of Prophet Ibrahim's devotion. Framing: "You're not just buying meat, you're following a prophet's example."

📅 Ramadan Fasting

Month-long training in self-control. Helps overcome present bias, instant gratification. Research shows Muslims smoke less, spend less during Ramadan.

Test Your Understanding

1. What did Daniel Kahneman win the Nobel Prize for?

A. Efficient markets theory
B. Rational choice theory
C. Integrating psychology into economics (cognitive biases)
D. Game theory
✅ Correct! Kahneman (with Tversky) pioneered behavioral economics, showing how humans systematically deviate from rationality.

2. What is loss aversion?

A. People prefer winning to losing
B. People don't care about losses
C. Losses and gains feel equal
D. Losses hurt about 2-2.5x more than equivalent gains please
✅ Correct! This asymmetry explains many financial behaviors—holding losers too long, selling winners too early.

3. Who won the 2017 Nobel Prize for contributions to behavioral economics?

A. Richard Thaler
B. Daniel Kahneman
C. Robert Shiller
D. Amos Tversky
✅ Correct! Thaler pioneered mental accounting, nudge theory, and showed how behavioral factors affect economic decisions.

4. What is the "bat and ball" problem designed to illustrate?

A. People are good at math
B. Fast System 1 thinking leads to intuitive but wrong answers
C. People are always rational
D. Slow System 2 is always correct
✅ Correct! Most people answer 10 cents (System 1), but correct is 5 cents (System 2). Shows fast thinking traps.

5. What is the endowment effect?

A. Rich people donate more
B. People value what they want more
C. People value things more once they own them
D. People don't value their possessions
✅ Correct! In Thaler's experiments, coffee mugs given to students were valued much higher by owners than buyers.

6. What does "nudge" theory recommend?

A. Small changes in choice architecture to improve decisions
B. Forcing people to make good choices
C. Removing all choices
D. Letting people make all decisions alone
✅ Correct! Nudges preserve freedom while steering people toward better choices (like opt-out retirement plans).

7. What is present bias?

A. Caring equally about present and future
B. Only caring about the future
C. Not caring about time at all
D. Overvaluing today relative to tomorrow
✅ Correct! Why we procrastinate, under-save, eat cake now—the future self is discounted.

8. How does Islam describe the self that follows base desires?

A. Nafs al-Mutma'innah
B. Nafs al-Ammarah
C. Nafs al-Lawwama
D. Ruh al-Qudus
✅ Correct! Nafs al-Ammarah is the commanding self that inclines toward evil and impulse.

9. Why do investors sell winners too early and hold losers too long?

A. They're rational
B. They follow expert advice
C. Loss aversion (realizing losses hurts more than gains please)
D. They don't care about profits
✅ Correct! This "disposition effect" is driven by loss aversion—selling winners feels good, selling losers admits mistake.

10. What does the Quran verse 50:16 teach about human nature?

A. Allah knows the whispers of our souls
B. Humans are perfectly rational
C. Humans have no inner thoughts
D. Allah is far from us
✅ Correct! "We know what his soul whispers to him, and We are closer to him than his jugular vein." Allah understands our deepest biases.

11. Why do defaults matter so much in choice architecture?

A. People love making choices
B. Defaults are illegal
C. People ignore defaults
D. People tend to stick with the default (inertia)
✅ Correct! Inertia and procrastination mean default options have huge effects—like organ donation rates from 15% to 90%+.

12. What Islamic practice helps counter present bias?

A. Eating more
B. Ramadan fasting (self-control training)
C. Sleeping late
D. Spending all savings
✅ Correct! Fasting teaches self-control, reducing present bias and impulsive behavior.

13. What is mental accounting?

A. Accounting for mental illness
B. Rational budgeting
C. Treating money differently based on mental categories
D. Ignoring money completely
✅ Correct! Thaler's concept—people treat $100 found differently than $100 earned, though money is fungible.

14. Why do lottery tickets sell even though expected value is negative?

A. People overweigh small probabilities
B. People are good at math
C. Lottery has positive expected value
D. People don't care about money
✅ Correct! Prospect theory shows we overweigh tiny probabilities (winning jackpot) and underweigh moderate ones.

15. What does tawakkul (trust in Allah) help overcome?

A. Overconfidence only
B. Loss aversion only
C. Present bias only
D. Anxiety about future, excessive fear, and greed
✅ Correct! Tawakkul brings peace, reducing the fear and greed that drive many behavioral biases.
