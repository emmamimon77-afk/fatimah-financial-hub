Beyond the Basics

In Lesson 7, we learned the fundamentals of fiscal policy. Now we dive deeper into the competing schools of thought, the mathematical models, historical case studies, and the complex politics of government spending and taxation.

The Great Debate: Keynesian vs. Supply-Side Economics

📈 Keynesian (Demand-Side)

John Maynard Keynes • 1936

  • Focus on aggregate demand
  • During recessions, government should spend more
  • Multiplier effect: $1 spending → >$1 GDP
  • Tax cuts for lower/middle class (they spend more)
  • Government can run deficits to stabilize economy

📊 Supply-Side

Arthur Laffer • 1970s

  • Focus on aggregate supply
  • Lower taxes to incentivize work, investment, production
  • Tax cuts for businesses and wealthy (they invest more)
  • Deregulation to reduce costs
  • Believe tax cuts can pay for themselves via growth

⚖️ The Debate in Practice

Keynesian View of 2017 Tax Cuts: Mostly benefited wealthy, increased deficit without much growth.

Supply-Side View of 2017 Tax Cuts: Boosted investment and brought manufacturing back.

Result: Growth was modest (2-3%), deficits expanded significantly. The debate continues.

The Multiplier Effect: How $1 Becomes >$1
Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC)

Where MPC = Marginal Propensity to Consume (fraction of extra income spent, not saved)

Worked Example

Government spends $100 million on infrastructure. MPC = 0.8 (people spend 80% of extra income).

Round 1: $100M → workers get paid → they spend $80M

Round 2: $80M → store owners get paid → they spend $64M

Round 3: $64M → and so on...

Total: $100M × (1 + 0.8 + 0.64 + 0.512 + ...) = $100M × 5 = $500M

Multiplier = 1/(1-0.8) = 5

✅ High Multiplier ( > 2 )

  • Infrastructure spending
  • Unemployment benefits
  • Aid to low-income families
  • During recessions (when MPC is high)

❌ Low Multiplier ( < 1 )

  • Tax cuts for wealthy (they save more)
  • Corporate tax cuts (may be saved or used for buybacks)
  • During booms (when MPC is low)
  • Imported goods (leakage from economy)

🏆 Empirical Estimates (2025)

  • Infrastructure spending: 1.5 - 2.5
  • Unemployment benefits: 1.6 - 2.2
  • Tax cuts for low-income: 1.0 - 1.5
  • Tax cuts for high-income: 0.3 - 0.6
  • Corporate tax cuts: 0.2 - 0.5
The Laffer Curve: Do Tax Cuts Pay for Themselves?

The Laffer Curve shows the relationship between tax rates and government revenue. At 0% tax, revenue = 0. At 100% tax, revenue = 0 (no one works). Somewhere in between lies the optimal rate that maximizes revenue.

📉 On the "Wrong Side"

If tax rates are above the optimal point, cutting rates could INCREASE revenue by incentivizing more work, investment, and reducing tax avoidance.

📈 On the "Right Side"

If tax rates are below the optimal point, cutting rates would DECREASE revenue. Most economists believe most developed countries are on the right side.

⚖️ The Reagan Experiment (1981-1986)

Reagan cut top marginal rate from 70% to 28%. Revenue initially fell, then rose as economy grew. Supply-siders claim victory. Critics note that payroll taxes increased and defense spending rose—the deficit exploded.

Kansas Experiment (2012-2017): Kansas cut income taxes dramatically. Economy didn't boom; revenue collapsed, forcing spending cuts in schools and infrastructure. Experiment reversed.

Ricardian Equivalence: Does Government Debt Matter?

🏆 Robert Barro • Harvard Economist

Revived David Ricardo's 19th-century idea: When government borrows, rational consumers save more because they expect future taxes to repay the debt. Result: Stimulus may have NO effect!

How It Works

Government cuts taxes by $1000, borrowing the money. Rational you thinks: "They'll have to raise taxes by $1000 + interest in the future. I'll save this $1000 to pay those future taxes." Net effect on spending = $0.

✅ Evidence FOR

  • Some studies show private savings rise with deficits
  • Countries with high debt don't see higher interest rates (Japan)
  • Long-term, stimulus effects seem to fade

❌ Evidence AGAINST

  • People are short-sighted (behavioral economics)
  • Liquidity-constrained consumers spend tax cuts
  • 2008-2009 stimulus did increase spending
  • Most evidence suggests partial Ricardian effects at best
Crowding Out: When Government Spending Hurts Private Investment

Crowding out occurs when government borrowing drives up interest rates, making it more expensive for businesses to invest.

📊 Direct Crowding Out

Government spends on roads → hires workers → fewer workers available for private sector → wages rise → private profits fall.

💰 Financial Crowding Out

Government borrows → demand for loans increases → interest rates rise → private investment becomes expensive → businesses invest less.

When Does Crowding Out Happen?

  • During booms: Severe crowding out (economy at full capacity)
  • During recessions: Little crowding out (idle resources, low rates)
  • Zero lower bound: Near-zero crowding out (2010-2020)
Case Study: The New Deal

📊 What Happened

FDR's response to Great Depression: Massive public works (CCC, WPA), Social Security, banking reforms, agricultural programs.

Spending: Federal spending rose from 3% of GDP to 10%.

Deficit: Peaked at 5% of GDP (small by modern standards).

⚖️ Did the New Deal End the Depression?

Keynesian View: Yes, but FDR didn't spend enough. Recovery was slow until WWII spending dwarfed New Deal.

Monetarist View (Friedman): New Deal policies actually prolonged Depression with price controls, anti-business rhetoric.

Historical Fact: Unemployment fell from 25% (1933) to 14% (1937), then spiked to 19% (1938) when FDR cut spending too early. Lesson: Premature austerity can cause double-dip recession.

Case Study: American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (2009)

📊 What Happened

Size: $831 billion (about 5% of GDP)

Components: Tax cuts (33%), state aid (33%), infrastructure/green energy (33%)

Timing: Passed February 2009, most spending 2009-2011

✅ CBO Estimate

Raised GDP by 2-4% in 2010, saved/created 2-3 million jobs. Multiplier estimates: 1.0 to 2.5 depending on component.

❌ Criticisms

Too small relative to crisis. Too much tax cutting (low multiplier). Too slow to spend. Did not fully offset state/local cuts.

Case Study: COVID-19 Fiscal Response

📊 What Happened

CARES Act (March 2020): $2.2 trillion (stimulus checks, unemployment, PPP loans)

Consolidated Appropriations Act (Dec 2020): $900 billion

American Rescue Plan (March 2021): $1.9 trillion

Total: ~$5 trillion (25% of GDP!) - largest fiscal response in history

✅ Successes

  • Poverty fell (stimulus checks)
  • Fastest recovery in history
  • Unemployment fell from 14.8% to 3.5% in 2 years
  • Business bankruptcies lower than expected

❌ Failures

  • Contributed to 2021-2024 inflation
  • Some PPP fraud ($80B estimated)
  • Debt-to-GDP jumped from 107% to 123%
  • Some argue it was "too much" stimulus

⚖️ The Inflation Debate

Larry Summers (warning): "This is the least responsible macroeconomic policy in 40 years" (Feb 2021). He was right about inflation.

Janet Yellen/Treasury: "Better to risk too much than too little."

Result: Inflation peaked at 9% (2022), forcing aggressive rate hikes. Debate continues on optimal size.

When Fiscal Policy Fails: Sovereign Debt Crises

🇬🇷 Greece (2010-2015)

Debt-to-GDP > 170%. Markets lost confidence. Interest rates > 30%. EU/IMF bailouts required. Austerity caused 25% GDP loss. Unemployment > 27%.

🇦🇷 Argentina (2001, 2018, 2023)

Repeated defaults. Chronic fiscal deficits. Loss of confidence. Currency crashes. Poverty spikes.

🇱🇰 Sri Lanka (2022)

Defaulted on foreign debt. Ran out of money for food, fuel, medicine. Government collapsed.

⚠️ Warning Signs of Debt Crisis

  • Persistent large deficits (5%+ of GDP)
  • Rising debt-to-GDP ratio
  • High foreign currency debt
  • Loss of investor confidence (rising yields)
  • Political instability
Islamic Public Finance: A Third Way

📜 Classical Islamic Fiscal System

  • Zakat: 2.5% wealth tax, redistributed to poor (8 categories in Quran)
  • Kharaj: Land tax (proportional, just)
  • Jizya: Protection tax for non-Muslims
  • Ushr: 10% tax on agricultural produce from owned land
  • Ghanimah: Spoils of war (20% to public treasury)
  • Waqf: Endowments for public goods (hospitals, schools, mosques)

🕌 Modern Islamic Fiscal Tools

  • Sukuk: Asset-backed public financing (instead of interest-bearing bonds)
  • Zakat funds: Separate state-managed poverty reduction
  • Waqf revitalization: Using old endowments for development
  • Islamic social finance: Zakat + Sadaqah + Qard Hasan

🌍 Country Examples

  • Malaysia: Sukuk for infrastructure (50% of government bonds)
  • Sudan: Interest-free treasury instruments
  • Indonesia: Waqf-based development projects
  • UAE: Dubai Islamic Bank finances public projects

How Would an Islamic Government Borrow?

Instead of issuing interest-bearing bonds (haram), an Islamic government could:

  • Issue Sukuk (sell shares in a public asset like a toll road)
  • Use Qard Hasan (interest-free loans from citizens, repaid at par)
  • Establish Waqf for specific projects (donors get ongoing reward)
  • Create development funds with profit-sharing (Mudaraba)

Justice in Taxation

Abu Yusuf (8th century judge) wrote in Kitab al-Kharaj: "Justice in taxation means that the burden should be proportional to ability to pay, and the collector should be kind and not harsh with the people."

Islamic principles require:

  • Progressive but fair rates
  • Transparency in collection and spending
  • Priority on basic needs (maqasid al-shariah)
  • No taxation without representation (shura)

1. What is the main difference between Keynesian and Supply-Side economics?

A. Keynesians focus on demand; Supply-Siders focus on incentives to produce
B. Keynesians want lower taxes; Supply-Siders want higher spending
C. They are the same thing
D. Keynesians hate business; Supply-Siders hate workers
✅ Correct! Keynesians focus on managing demand; Supply-Siders focus on removing barriers to production (taxes, regulation).

2. What is the multiplier formula?

A. 1/(1-MPC)
B. 1/MPC
C. MPC/(1-MPC)
D. 1-MPC
✅ Correct! Multiplier = 1/(1-MPC). If MPC = 0.8, multiplier = 5.

3. What does the Laffer Curve show?

A. Relationship between tax rates and government revenue
B. Relationship between spending and inflation
C. Relationship between debt and growth
D. Relationship between unemployment and GDP
✅ Correct! The Laffer Curve shows that tax revenue is zero at 0% and 100% rates, with an optimal point in between.

4. What is Ricardian Equivalence?

A. When consumers save more because they expect future taxes to repay debt
B. When government spending creates inflation
C. When tax cuts always increase revenue
D. When deficits don't matter
✅ Correct! Ricardian Equivalence suggests that rational consumers save tax cuts to pay future taxes, neutralizing stimulus.
