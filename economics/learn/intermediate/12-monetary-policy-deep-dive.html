The Taylor Rule: A Formula for Interest Rates

🏆 John Taylor • Stanford Economist

In 1993, John Taylor proposed a simple formula that describes how central banks should set interest rates based on economic conditions. It became the most famous monetary policy rule in modern economics.

i = r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*)

Where:

  • i = target federal funds rate
  • r* = neutral real interest rate (usually 2%)
  • π = current inflation rate
  • π* = target inflation rate (usually 2%)
  • y - y* = output gap (actual GDP minus potential GDP)

Worked Example (2025)

Assumptions: r* = 2%, π = 3.2%, π* = 2%, output gap = -1% (slightly below potential)

i = 2 + 3.2 + 0.5(3.2 - 2) + 0.5(-1)

i = 2 + 3.2 + 0.5(1.2) - 0.5

i = 2 + 3.2 + 0.6 - 0.5 = 5.3%

The rule suggests rates should be about 5.3%. The actual Fed funds rate in 2025 was 5.25-5.5%—remarkably close!

⚖️ Rules vs. Discretion: The Great Debate

Milton Friedman (Rules): Central banks should follow fixed rules to prevent political interference and time-inconsistency problems. "K percent rule" for money supply growth.

Keynesians (Discretion): Central banks need flexibility to respond to unforeseen crises (2008, 2020). Rigid rules can't handle every situation.

Modern Synthesis: Most central banks use Taylor-type rules as guidelines but retain discretion—"constrained discretion."

The Fed's Balance Sheet: A Window into QE

Before 2008, the Fed's balance sheet was about $900 billion. By 2025, it peaked at nearly $9 trillion! Let's understand what this means.

📊 Fed Assets Over Time

  • 2007: $0.9 trillion (pre-crisis)
  • 2014: $4.5 trillion (post-QE1, QE2, QE3)
  • 2020: $7.2 trillion (pandemic response)
  • 2022: $9.0 trillion (peak)
  • 2025: $7.5 trillion (quantitative tightening underway)

📈 Assets (What the Fed Owns)

  • Treasury securities (government bonds)
  • Mortgage-backed securities (MBS)
  • Emergency loans (during crises)

📉 Liabilities (What the Fed Owes)

  • Currency in circulation
  • Bank reserves (electronic money banks hold at Fed)
  • Reverse repos (temporary borrowing)

Why Does Balance Sheet Size Matter?

When the Fed buys bonds (QE), it creates bank reserves (liabilities). More reserves = more money in the banking system = potential for inflation and asset bubbles. When the Fed sells bonds (QT), it drains reserves.

Quantitative Easing: How It Really Works

QE1 (2008-2010): $1.75 trillion

Response to financial crisis. Bought mortgage-backed securities to stabilize housing market and banks.

QE2 (2010-2011): $600 billion

Bought Treasury securities to lower long-term rates and support recovery.

QE3 (2012-2014): $85 billion/month

Open-ended purchases until labor market improved. Ended when unemployment fell to 6.5%.

QE4 (2020-2022): $120 billion/month

Pandemic response. Bought $80B Treasuries + $40B MBS monthly. Balance sheet peaked at $9T.

⚠️ Criticisms of QE

  • Inequality: Asset price inflation benefits the wealthy (who own stocks/homes)
  • Moral hazard: Encourages excessive risk-taking
  • Hard to exit: Markets become addicted to easy money
  • Distorts markets: Artificially low rates misallocate capital
Forward Guidance: Talking Markets into Submission

Forward guidance is when central banks communicate their future policy intentions to influence market expectations.

Historical Examples

2013: "Taper Tantrum" - Fed Chair Bernanke suggested QE would slow. Markets panicked, yields spiked. Lesson: Communication matters!

2020-2022: Fed promised to keep rates near zero until inflation reached 2% AND was on track to exceed 2% for some time. "Average inflation targeting."

2025-2026: Fed used forward guidance to signal rate cuts as inflation moderated, easing financial conditions before actually cutting.

The Psychology of Forward Guidance

If businesses believe rates will stay low, they invest more. If consumers believe inflation will stay low, they don't demand big wage increases. Expectations become self-fulfilling.

Case Study: The Volcker Shock (1979-1982)

🏆 Paul Volcker • Fed Chair 1979-1987

When Volcker took office, inflation was 14%. He raised the federal funds rate to 20%—the highest in US history—causing a severe recession but permanently breaking inflation expectations.

📉 The Pain

  • Unemployment peaked at 10.8%
  • GDP fell sharply (1982 recession)
  • Farmers protested with tractors on Washington mall
  • Auto and housing industries devastated

📈 The Gain

  • Inflation fell from 14% to 3%
  • Credibility restored for decades
  • Paved way for 1980s boom
  • Lesson learned: Central banks must act decisively

Why Volcker Succeeded

He convinced markets he would do "whatever it takes." Once expectations shifted, inflation fell with less pain than many predicted. This is the power of credibility.

Case Study: ECB and the Euro Crisis (2010-2015)

🇬🇷 Greece's Crisis

  • Debt-to-GDP > 170%
  • Interest rates spiked > 30%
  • EU/IMF bailouts required
  • Greece suffered depression-level GDP loss

🏛️ ECB's Response

  • LTRO: 3-year loans to banks (€1 trillion)
  • OMT: "Whatever it takes" (Mario Draghi, 2012)
  • Negative rates: 2014-2022
  • QE: Started 2015, continued for years

⚖️ Was the Euro Crisis a Monetary Policy Failure?

Critics say the ECB was too slow to act. Defenders say the ECB lacked a fiscal union to back it. The crisis revealed the limits of monetary policy without fiscal coordination.

Modern Monetary Theory (MMT): The Controversial Alternative

💡 What is MMT?

Countries that issue their own currency (like USA, Japan, UK) cannot go bankrupt. They can always create more money to pay debts. The only constraint is inflation, not solvency.

✅ MMT Claims

  • Taxes don't fund spending—they regulate demand
  • Government can spend freely until inflation hits
  • Job guarantee can maintain full employment

❌ Critics Respond

  • Ignore political constraints on central banks
  • Hyperinflation risk (Zimbabwe, Venezuela)
  • 2021-2023 inflation may prove MMT wrong

MMT in Practice?

MMT advocates pointed to Japan's high debt (260% of GDP) with low inflation as proof. Critics point to 2022-2024 global inflation as counter-proof. The debate continues.

Islamic Monetary Policy: An Interest-Free Framework

📜 Principles of Islamic Monetary Policy

  • No Riba: Interest-based tools (Fed funds rate, QE) are prohibited
  • Asset-backing: Money creation must be tied to real assets
  • Profit-sharing: Instead of interest, use Mudaraba and Musharaka
  • Real economy focus: Money for productive investment, not speculation

🕌 Islamic Monetary Tools

  • Mudaraba certificates: Central bank issues profit-sharing instruments
  • Commodity murabaha: Central bank buys/sells commodities to manage liquidity
  • Reserve requirements: Can be used (no riba issue)
  • Moral suasion: Guidance to banks within Shariah framework

🌍 Countries Experimenting

  • Malaysia: Dual system with Islamic money market
  • Sudan: Fully interest-free banking since 1980s
  • Iran: Islamic banking law (though debated)
  • GCC countries: Developing Islamic monetary frameworks

How Would an Islamic Central Bank Fight Inflation?

Instead of raising interest rates (haram), an Islamic central bank could:

  • Increase reserve requirements
  • Issue profit-sharing certificates to absorb excess liquidity
  • Use moral suasion to encourage lower profit rates
  • Regulate credit growth through macroprudential tools
  • In extreme cases, commodity market interventions
