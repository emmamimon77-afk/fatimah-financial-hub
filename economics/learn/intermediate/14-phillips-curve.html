The Phillips Curve

Inflation silently deprives people of their money's value. Understanding its causes helps us prevent this form of economic injustice.

The Most Famous Relationship in Macroeconomics

In 1958, New Zealand economist A.W. Phillips discovered a striking pattern in UK data: when unemployment was low, wages rose quickly; when unemployment was high, wages were stable. This relationship—the Phillips Curve—became the cornerstone of macroeconomic policy for decades.

📚 The Phillips Curve: An inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. Lower unemployment → higher inflation. Higher unemployment → lower inflation.

🏆 A.W. Phillips • 1914-1975

New Zealand economist who plotted 100 years of UK data (1861-1957) and found a remarkably stable curve. His discovery changed how central banks think forever.

The Original Phillips Curve (1958)

The original relationship: lower unemployment, higher wage inflation

Intuition

Low unemployment (3-4%): Workers are scarce. Companies compete for labor by raising wages. Workers have more bargaining power. Inflation rises.

High unemployment (8-10%): Workers are plentiful. Companies don't need to raise wages. Workers have little bargaining power. Inflation stays low.

The Policy Trade-off: Can We Choose?

📈 Low Unemployment (3-4%)

Cost: Higher inflation (5-6%)

Benefit: Everyone who wants a job has one

📉 Low Inflation (2%)

Cost: Higher unemployment (6-7%)

Benefit: Money retains its value

In the 1960s, policymakers believed they could permanently choose any point on the curve. Want 3% unemployment? Accept 5% inflation. Want 2% inflation? Accept 6% unemployment. It seemed like a stable menu of choices.

⚖️ The Kennedy Tax Cuts (1964)

President Kennedy explicitly used Phillips Curve reasoning: tax cuts would stimulate demand, lower unemployment, and the resulting inflation was an acceptable price. It worked—unemployment fell, growth boomed, inflation rose modestly.

The Great Breakdown: Stagflation (1970s)

📉 1973-1975: Everything Changed

Oil prices quadrupled (OPEC embargo). Both inflation AND unemployment rose simultaneously—something the Phillips Curve said couldn't happen.

Inflation: 12% | Unemployment: 9% | Result: "Stagflation" (stagnation + inflation)

📊 1960s (Normal)

  • Unemployment: 4% → Inflation: 2%
  • Unemployment: 3% → Inflation: 4%
  • Trade-off worked

📉 1970s (Stagflation)

  • Unemployment: 6% → Inflation: 8%
  • Unemployment: 9% → Inflation: 12%
  • Trade-off broke

What Happened?

The old Phillips Curve assumed people didn't expect inflation. Once people started expecting inflation, behavior changed. Workers demanded higher wages. Companies raised prices preemptively. The relationship shifted.

Milton Friedman's Revolution: Expectations Matter

🏆 Milton Friedman • Nobel Prize 1976

Friedman argued there is NO long-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment. In the long run, unemployment returns to its "natural rate" (NAIRU), regardless of inflation.

π = πᵉ - β(u - uⁿ) + ε

Where:

  • π = actual inflation
  • πᵉ = expected inflation
  • u = actual unemployment
  • uⁿ = natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU)
  • β = sensitivity of inflation to unemployment gap
  • ε = supply shocks (like oil prices)

Intuition

Short run: If unemployment falls below natural rate (u < uⁿ), inflation rises ABOVE expectations. Workers are surprised by higher prices.

Long run: Workers adjust expectations. They demand higher wages. Unemployment returns to natural rate. Inflation stays higher. No permanent gain.

📈 Short-Run Phillips Curve

Downward sloping (trade-off exists temporarily)

Shifts with expectations

📊 Long-Run Phillips Curve

Vertical at natural rate (uⁿ)

No trade-off permanently

NAIRU: The Natural Rate

NAIRU = Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment

The unemployment rate at which inflation is stable. If unemployment falls below NAIRU, inflation rises. If unemployment rises above NAIRU, inflation falls.

📈 Below NAIRU

u = 3%, NAIRU = 5%

Labor market tight → wages rise → inflation accelerates

📉 Above NAIRU

u = 7%, NAIRU = 5%

Labor market slack → wages stable → inflation decelerates

📊 Estimated NAIRU Over Time

  • 1960s: ~4%
  • 1970s-80s: ~6% (baby boomers, women entering workforce)
  • 1990s-2000s: ~5%
  • 2010s: ~4.5% (aging workforce, fewer new entrants)
  • 2020s: ~4% (post-pandemic estimates vary)
The Great Moderation & The Flattening Phillips Curve

📊 1985-2007: The Great Moderation

Inflation and unemployment both fell. The Phillips Curve seemed to disappear! From 1992-2007, unemployment fell from 7.5% to 4.5%, yet inflation stayed around 2-3%.

Why Did the Curve Flatten?

  • Anchored expectations: People trusted the Fed to keep inflation low
  • Globalization: Cheap imports from China kept prices down
  • Technology: Productivity gains without wage pressure
  • Union decline: Workers lost bargaining power
  • Central bank credibility: Volcker/Greenspan era

⚖️ Is the Phillips Curve Dead?

Yes (critics): 2010-2019 showed no relationship. Unemployment fell from 10% to 3.5%, inflation barely moved.

No (defenders): 2021-2024 showed it's alive! When unemployment fell to 3.4%, inflation surged to 9%. The curve still exists, but it's flatter.

Consensus: The curve is flatter than in the 1970s, but not dead. Supply shocks (COVID, war) still matter more.

Supply Shocks: When the Curve Shifts

📉 Negative Supply Shock

Example: Oil price spike (1973, 1979, 2022)

Inflation ↑, Unemployment ↑ → Stagflation

Curve shifts UP and RIGHT

📈 Positive Supply Shock

Example: Technology boom, China joining WTO

Inflation ↓, Unemployment ↓ → "Goldilocks"

Curve shifts DOWN and LEFT

🛢️ 2022 Oil Price Shock

Russia-Ukraine war caused energy and food prices to spike. Inflation surged to 9% even as unemployment was low (3.6%). Classic negative supply shock—the worst of both worlds.

Case Study: The Volcker Disinflation (1979-1982)

📉 What Happened

Fed Chair Paul Volcker raised rates to 20% to break inflation. Unemployment soared to 10.8%. Inflation fell from 14% to 3%.

Sacrifice Ratio: How much GDP lost to reduce inflation by 1%? Estimated: 2-3% GDP per 1% inflation reduction.

The Cost of Credibility

To reduce inflation from 14% to 4% (10% reduction):

Sacrifice ratio 2 → 20% of GDP lost (~$4 trillion in today's economy)

But once credibility was established, future disinflations cost less. By 1990, the Fed could lower inflation with minimal pain because everyone believed they would.

Case Study: The 2021-2024 Inflation Surge

📈 What Happened

  • 2021: Stimulus + supply chains + reopening → inflation 7%
  • 2022: War in Ukraine → energy/food shock → inflation 9%
  • 2023-2024: Fed raised rates to 5.5% → inflation fell to 3%

✅ Phillips Curve Alive?

  • Unemployment fell to 3.4% (lowest in 50 years)
  • Inflation surged to 9%
  • Classic negative relationship appeared!

⚠️ But Supply Shocks Dominated

  • Energy prices: +40%
  • Food prices: +25%
  • Used cars: +50% (supply chain)
  • Curve shifted, not just moved along

⚖️ Was It Demand or Supply?

Team Demand: Too much stimulus caused overheating (Summers).

Team Supply: Mostly supply shocks (oil, food, chips).

Consensus: Both. Demand pulled inflation up, supply shocks pushed it higher.

What This Means for Policymakers

🎯 Short Run (1-2 years)

  • Trade-off exists temporarily
  • Can lower unemployment by accepting higher inflation
  • But only until expectations adjust

🎯 Long Run (3+ years)

  • No trade-off
  • Unemployment returns to natural rate
  • Inflation determined by money growth & expectations
  • Central bank must anchor expectations

The Most Important Lesson

Expectations are everything. If people believe the central bank will control inflation, they won't demand huge wage increases, and businesses won't raise prices preemptively. This is why "inflation targeting" works—it anchors expectations.

Islamic Perspective: Inflation as Injustice

📜 Quranic Principles

  • "Give full measure and weight" (6:152): Inflation that erodes money's value can be seen as a form of cheating, if contracts don't account for it.
  • "Do not consume wealth unjustly" (2:188): Unanticipated inflation transfers wealth from savers to borrowers without consent—potentially unjust.
  • "And do not deprive people of their due" (26:183): Inflation silently deprives people of purchasing power.

💰 Zakat and Inflation

Zakat is 2.5% of savings annually. High inflation can erode wealth before Zakat is due. Scholars debate: Should Zakat be based on original value or current purchasing power?

📈 Riba and Inflation

Conventional loans charge interest partly to compensate for inflation. In Islam, this is not permitted. Instead, Islamic finance uses profit-sharing (Mudaraba) where returns reflect real economic performance, not just inflation compensation.

Inflation Adjustment in Islamic Contracts

Some modern Islamic scholars permit inflation-indexed contracts to maintain justice. For example, a loan repayment could be adjusted for inflation to preserve real value—as long as it's not structured as interest. This is debated but increasingly accepted in high-inflation environments.

The Justice Dimension

Inflation hurts the poor most (they hold cash, no assets). It benefits the wealthy (they own stocks, real estate). This concentration of wealth violates the Quranic principle that wealth "should not circulate only among the rich" (59:7). Islamic economists argue for policies that protect the vulnerable from inflation's harshest effects.

Test Your Understanding

1. What did A.W. Phillips discover in 1958?

A. An inverse relationship between unemployment and wage inflation
B. A positive relationship between GDP and inflation
C. That money supply determines prices
D. That trade deficits cause recessions
✅ Correct! Phillips found that when unemployment was low, wages rose quickly—the inverse relationship.

2. What is "stagflation"?

A. High inflation + high unemployment simultaneously
B. Low inflation + low unemployment
C. High growth + high inflation
D. Deflation + recession
✅ Correct! Stagflation = stagnation (high unemployment) + inflation. The 1970s broke the simple Phillips Curve.

3. What did Milton Friedman add to the Phillips Curve?

A. Inflation expectations matter; no long-run trade-off
B. The curve is always stable
C. Government spending determines everything
D. The curve only applies to developing countries
✅ Correct! Friedman's expectations-augmented Phillips Curve showed that any trade-off is temporary—in the long run, unemployment returns to its natural rate.

4. What is NAIRU?

A. The unemployment rate where inflation is stable
B. The rate where inflation is zero
C. Maximum unemployment possible
D. Minimum unemployment possible
✅ Correct! NAIRU = Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment. Below it, inflation rises; above it, inflation falls.

5. What happened during the "Great Moderation" (1985-2007)?

A. Both inflation and unemployment fell together
B. Inflation rose, unemployment fell
C. Inflation fell, unemployment rose
D. Both rose together
✅ Correct! Both fell—the Phillips Curve seemed to disappear, puzzling economists.

6. What is a negative supply shock?

A. Something that raises costs and shifts Phillips Curve up/right
B. Something that lowers costs
C. A tax cut
D. More immigration
✅ Correct! Oil shocks, wars, supply chain disruptions raise both inflation and unemployment—the worst outcome.

7. What was the "sacrifice ratio" in the Volcker disinflation?

A. About 2-3% GDP lost per 1% inflation reduction
B. 0% GDP loss
C. 10% GDP loss per 1% inflation
D. GDP increased
✅ Correct! Volcker's fight cost about 2-3% of GDP per point of inflation reduction—a painful but necessary lesson.

8. In the 2021-2024 inflation surge, what was the lowest unemployment rate?

A. 3.4%
B. 5%
C. 7%
D. 10%
✅ Correct! Unemployment fell to 3.4% in 2023—the lowest in 50 years—while inflation surged.

9. What does the long-run Phillips Curve look like?

A. Vertical at the natural rate of unemployment
B. Downward sloping
C. Upward sloping
D. Horizontal
✅ Correct! In the long run, no trade-off exists—unemployment returns to natural rate regardless of inflation.

10. Why is anchoring inflation expectations important?

A. If people expect low inflation, they won't fuel wage-price spiral
B. It makes the Phillips Curve steeper
C. It increases unemployment permanently
D. It has no effect
✅ Correct! Anchored expectations prevent self-fulfilling inflation spirals. This is why central banks target 2%—to shape expectations.

11. What Quranic principle relates to inflation's injustice?

A. "Give full measure and weight" (6:152)
B. "Fight in the way of Allah" (2:190)
C. "And establish prayer" (2:43)
D. "And speak kindly" (2:83)
✅ Correct! Inflation that erodes value without consent can be seen as a form of cheating or injustice.

12. What was the peak US inflation rate in 2022?

A. 9%
B. 5%
C. 12%
D. 3%
✅ Correct! Inflation peaked at 9% in June 2022, the highest since 1981.

13. How might Islamic contracts address inflation?

A. Inflation-indexed adjustments (debated, but some scholars permit for justice)
B. Adding interest to compensate
C. Ignoring it completely
D. Only using cash
✅ Correct! Some modern scholars permit inflation adjustments to maintain justice, as long as it's not structured as riba.

14. What causes the short-run Phillips Curve to shift?

A. Changes in inflation expectations or supply shocks
B. Movement along the curve
C. Changes in the money supply only
D. Government spending changes
✅ Correct! Expectations and supply shocks shift the curve; demand changes move along it.
